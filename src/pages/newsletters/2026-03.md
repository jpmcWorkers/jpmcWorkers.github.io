<h3>Rights Reminder
</h3>
<p>Worker rights are protected by the National Labor Relations Act (NLRA) of 1935.
</p>
<p>According to this federal law, the following actions are legally protected rights:
</p>
<ul>
    <li>speaking about pay
    </li>
    <li>discussing working conditions
    </li>
    <li>talking about any and all terms and conditions of employment
    </li>
    <li>forming, joining, supporting, and chatting about unions
    </li>
    <li>any joint action for mutual aid and protection
    </li>
</ul>
<p>Further, this act outlaws retaliation, interference, and a variety of other unfair labor practices.
</p>
<p>Many of us were told this year not to discuss our compensation numbers. Indeed, in a recent widely assigned training, one of the assessment questions implies that discussing those numbers is a violation of company policy.
</p>
<p><strong><em>Such a company policy would be a violation of federal law!</em></strong>
</p>
<p>The company is subject to the law, not the other way around.<strong></strong>
</p>
<p>On a side note, this is why we in the union encourage the use of the "Bank Business" time code for any weather related working from home. If the local authorities declare driving illegal due to weather, you didn't decide to WFH. You are simply following the law.
</p>

<h3>Steward's Corner
</h3>
<p>A prospective member anonymously reported numerous operational and policy problems at an Illinois-area Lockbox site. Key allegations include:
</p>
<ul>
    <li>Shifting, undocumented, and archaic production tracking practices
    </li>
    <li>Cut-throat competition over metrics, promoting overwork and corrupt record-keeping
    </li>
    <li>A few slow time clocks for too many employees, leading to inaccurate attendance records and unfair warnings
    </li>
    <li>Inadequate supplies necessary for job performance, and blaming employees for this employer failure
    </li>
    <li>Despite the "Clean Desk" policy preventing reliance on paper, procedural updates are rarely shared electronically
    </li>
    <li>Hiring, firing, and job-assignment practices are opaque and seem prone to favoritism and misrepresentation.
    </li>
</ul>
<p>We the members of the JPMC Workers Alliance demand justice for these workers!
</p>
<p>Union recognition and collective bargaining would ensure fair worker treatment and compensation under sane, standardized procedures and consistent management.
</p>
<p>If you see similar problems in your workplace, get in touch. We have people all over the company who building worker power to apply corrective pressure.
</p>

<h3>Grievances and Results in Elgin
</h3>
<p>On 19 February, a member in Elgin, Illinois shared a grievance.
</p>
<p>In his department, bathroom access outside of pre-scheduled breaks was indirectly limited to seven minutes per day. Using the bathroom had been labeled "discretionary time" and was therefore subject to disciplinary action for overages.
</p>
<p>Other Union members thought this policy to be bizarre and probably a violation of an OSHA safety code, and recommended a complaint accordingly.
</p>
<p> As of 24 February, a two-page warning letter from OSHA is posted in the facility identifying the specific regulation, stating the nature of the alleged violation, and demanding the company correct itself or face a full OSHA inspection. The letter states that OSHA received the allegation on 11 February, 8 days before the union at large learned of the problem.
</p>
<p> At this time it's unclear whether whoever filed the initial OSHA report is the same person or even associated with the Union, but we are monitoring the situation and will take appropriate action to keep OSHA updated on the situation.
</p>
<p> We respect the company's desire to maintain productivity while hourly employees are on-the-clock, but we also believe that it is unhealthy, unsafe, and even unproductive to constrain bathroom access in a punitive manner.
</p>
<p> Let us rather assume good faith by default and address outliers on a case-by-case basis with both compassion and common sense.
</p>

<h3>Organizing Poetry from BK
</h3>
<p>Note: Based on a true story that took place at a Chase call center in Katy, TX in February.
</p>
<p><br>
</p>
<h5><em>It Started in the Hallway</em>
</h5>
<div>No speeches.
</div>
<div>No big announcement.
</div>
<div>Just conversations.
</div>
<div>At a recent workplace event, coworkers stopped. Listened. Asked questions.
</div>
<div>Many hadn’t heard much about the law that protects our right to organize. So it came up naturally:
</div>
<div>In 1935, workers won the legal right to stand together. That right still exists. It’s federal law.
</div>
<div><br>
</div>
<div>For most, that was new.
</div>
<div>One small group pulled another coworker over:
</div>
<div><br>
</div>
<div>“Come here. This is important.”
</div>
<div><br>
</div>
<div>That’s the moment.
</div>
<div>Not pressure.
</div>
<div>Not politics.
</div>
<div>Learning.
</div>
<div>Connecting civil rights history to workplace rights.
</div>
<div>Realizing we don’t have to navigate everything alone.
</div>
<div>Understanding that collective action isn’t radical &mdash; it’s protected.
</div>
<div>For those already involved: this is why we show up.
</div>
<div>For those watching quietly: you’re not the only one thinking about it.
</div>
<div>The conversations are happening.
</div>
<div>Come see.
</div>
