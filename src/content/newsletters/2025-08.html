Happy August!

We did it! We hosted another successful event, the Summer Splash at Polaris!

We served approximately 450 ice cream sandwiches and 5 whole watermelons!

A conservative estimate for the number of attendees is 400, at least 100 more than attended the pizza party a few months ago. Although this may feel like a small increase, there are two factors worth considering.

First, this event was clearly advertised as a union event. We even heard a couple of people say that their manager had told the team they couldn’t attend. That is a violation of the National Labor Relations Act (section 8(a)(1)), but how many of us employees are aware of that?

Second, this event was held outside, on the opposite side of the building from where most people sit. The pizza party was directly next to many people’s desks, and even those who had not seen signs saw the piles of pizza pass by, tempting them to follow.

The fact that we saw more people show up despite these two factors is a huge win!

Of course, we know there is always room to improve, and we have learned some valuable lessons.


Lessons Learned

Our biggest lesson was simply around time and numbers. A handful of us did all of the work for this event in two weeks.

While that clearly works to some extent, it leads to some of us pushing ourselves too hard and then having to take some time away from organizing to rest.

We cannot treat unionizing a company this huge as a sprint, or even a series of sprints. It is a marathon, or perhaps more accurately, a very long relay race.

Truthfully, the event would have been a bigger success if more people were involved in planning and preparation.

Particularly, there were not enough people hanging posters and folding booklets.

Of course, having plenty of people hanging posters does very little when they are actively being removed.

To help with this, we have talked about a staggered lunch, where the first shift hangs posters and following shifts keep an eye out until those posters are removed. If the removal of posters can be recorded and shown to be a violation of the NLRA, we can open an unfair labor practice violation.

We’ve also learned that frozen handouts are difficult. Until we have the resources to do them properly, we will stick to handouts that are less temperature sensitive.

Further, at our next event, we will have some clicker counters. This allows for a comfortably anonymous number of attendees, so we can report our successes more accurately.

From our very own Nic Welch:

Be mindful of your own energy... you're no good to us dead! This is a MASSIVE task, if it was easy everyone would be doing it. Be able to talk about the benefits of unionization, be able to confirm that it is LEGAL and PROTECTED and that you have the absolute right to discuss working conditions with your coworkers and to advocate for yourself. Talk about bargaining units, share ideas on how to establish such a thing. Participate as much or as little as you like... but DO SOMETHING!

Note: One of those working conditions we’re allowed to discuss is salary. No matter how “rude” your managers may call it, that discussion adds transparency and a point of reference. This lets people get a better idea of whether there are discriminatory practices happening in the setting of salaries.


Union Dues

The two most important pieces of information are:

  1. Dues only start after we vote to ratify our union contract.

  2. When we vote on our union contract, we will vote for pay improvements more than sufficient to cover the dues.

To reiterate, union dues are NOT applicable until we agree on a contract, and we will include a raise. This means union dues are part of a pay increase, and will not reduce the amount you currently bring home to your family.

We are working with the Communication Workers of America, so the dues rate will be 1.5%. Most likely, we will vote for at least a 10% raise, although the exact amount is up to us.

But why would you want to pay union dues?

Besides all of the day-to-day benefits that come with a union, such as higher pay and a return to flexibility, another reason to pay union dues is for financial security.

Should the company force a strike, accumulated union dues are used to keep striking workers fed and housed.

If a union member is unjustly fired, union dues are used to help that worker stay afloat through any legal proceedings.

Again, we do not have union dues yet, and will not have them until we have a contract fully negotiated, agreed upon, ratified, and enacted.

Contract negotiations will not even begin until after the first bargaining unit has won its election to officially unionize. And an election doesn't happen until we gather enough support to trigger it (this is what we mean by “critical mass”).

As you can imagine, we are still a very long way away from collecting mandatory union dues from anybody’s paycheck!


Financial Contributions

Until then, any and all financial contributions to our efforts are strictly voluntary.

Indeed, if your budget is tight, I personally encourage you not to donate until you can do so without jeopardizing your own finances. If your budget has some leeway, however, please consider allocating some of it to our cause, to help fund more fun and engaging events like the Summer Splash, and to cover operational costs like printing and legal fees.

We said in the special edition newsletter two weeks ago that we would have a contribution link ready. Unfortunately, we have hit another legal snag.

While we are incorporated with the state of Ohio, we do not yet have an IRS tax status. Specifically, we are registering with the IRS as a 501(c)(5) labor organization.

Once that registration is complete, we will be able to send out a contribution link to all of you lovely people.


Our Mission

Regardless of legal status, we are an organization of, by, and for the workers of JPMC.

Our primary mission is to improve working conditions for all JPMC workers -- eventually through a legal mandate for collective bargaining, but first through advocacy and every other legitimate means at our disposal.

The foundation for this mission has four layers.

First, awareness. The workers must know that we exist, we are real, and we are not giving up until we have won.

Second, education. We distribute literature that informs employees of our rights and how to exercise them, in order to help us improve our working conditions.

Third, participation. We're blessed with a few people willing to do a lot, but we also need a lot of people willing to do a little. To that end, here's a list of little ways to help the cause.

Fourth, resources. Unionizing is fulfilling work, but producing educational materials and throwing events can’t be done for free. That’s why we’re setting up a better way for everyone to financially contribute to the cause.
